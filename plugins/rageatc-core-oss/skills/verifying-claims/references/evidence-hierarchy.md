# Evidence Hierarchy and Weighting

Tier detail and context-dependent weighting behind Phase 4. Tiering test: original research or data → primary; analysis or synthesis of primary sources → secondary; compilation or index → tertiary.

## Tier 1 — Primary sources

Original documents of events, discoveries, or research; first-hand accounts or original data collection.

**Examples by domain:**

- **Academic:** original peer-reviewed research papers, dissertations, original datasets, clinical trial registries and results
- **Government:** official statistics (ONS, Eurostat, census), legislation and regulations, court rulings, parliamentary records, agency reports with original data collection
- **Historical:** archives and original documents, letters and diaries, period photographs and artefacts, original recordings, archaeological evidence
- **Corporate/technical:** patents and specifications, official financial statements, manufacturer documentation, official statements, white papers reporting original data
- **Media:** original investigative reporting with first-hand sources, journalist-conducted interviews, eyewitness accounts, original photographs and video

**Weighting nuance beyond the SKILL.md bands:**

- A single primary source without corroboration → Likely (70–85%), not Confirmed
- Primary sources disagreeing → Disputed; investigate methodological differences and seek additional sources

**Practice:** cite primary sources directly when they exist; trace secondary sources back to the primaries they cite; read the full source, not excerpts; check methodology — primaries can be flawed too. Classic mistake: citing a newspaper's account of ONS statistics instead of the ONS bulletin itself.

## Tier 2 — Secondary sources

Analysis, interpretation, or synthesis of primary sources.

**Examples:** literature reviews, systematic reviews and meta-analyses, academic textbooks, review articles; reputable news reporting (Reuters, AP, BBC, FT) and investigative journalism with source attribution; think tank reports citing primary research, professional position papers, policy and technical analysis by qualified experts; high-quality subject reference works.

**Weighting nuance:**

- A single secondary source → Possible (50–69%)
- Secondary sources disagreeing → trace back to the primaries to resolve

**Legitimate uses:** primaries inaccessible; technical primaries needing expert interpretation; synthesis across many primaries; context and background; orientation toward the right primaries.

**Quality markers:** cites primary sources, transparent methodology, relevant author expertise, editorial or peer review, working corrections policy. **Red flags:** no primary citations, uncredentialed author, contradicts the primaries it cites, cherry-picks evidence.

## Tier 3 — Tertiary sources

Indexes, compilations, and summaries without new analysis: encyclopaedias (including Wikipedia), dictionaries, almanacs, aggregators, listicles, social media summaries, infographics without original data.

**Never sufficient for verification or formal citation.** Use for orientation, terminology, and finding the primaries and secondaries — then cite those. Not "According to Wikipedia, X" but "According to [the primary source Wikipedia cites], X".

## Context-dependent weighting

Directional adjustments applied through qualitative judgement — not arithmetic; the SKILL.md confidence bands and their criteria govern.

**Raise weight when:** multiple genuinely independent sources agree; the source has a strong track record (clean fact-check history, corrects errors, cited by experts); recency matters for the topic and the source is recent; methodology is transparent and rigorous (pre-registration, open data, peer review); conflicts of interest are disclosed; the claim sits within the author's domain of expertise.

**Lower weight when:** history of errors, retractions, or failed fact-checks; undisclosed conflicts of interest; methodology unclear or flawed (if badly flawed, do not rely on it at all); a single source without corroboration; outdated for a fast-moving field; the author is outside their domain; RADAR shows high bias or anonymity. A source irrelevant to the claim is excluded, not down-weighted.

## Special cases

**Pre-prints:** original research without peer review — between tiers. Treat as Possible-to-Likely; raise if the authors are established experts and the methodology is sound, lower for novel uncorroborated claims. Note pre-print status explicitly and check whether a peer-reviewed version has since appeared.

**Tier 1 vs Tier 2 conflict:** confirm both address the same question and the primary is not outdated; check whether the secondaries independently rest on different primaries or all cite the same one; examine methodological differences. Generally prefer a high-quality, current Tier 1 — and surface the disagreement either way. If multiple independent secondaries contradict a single primary, investigate that primary rather than assuming it wins.

**Grey literature** (government reports, think tank publications, industry white papers, NGO research): tier by function — original data collection → Tier 1; analysis of existing data → Tier 2; compilation → Tier 3. Weight by the organisation: government agencies with transparent methodology high; think tanks moderate (check funding); industry white papers lower (conflicts of interest); NGO reports moderate (assess methodology and bias).
