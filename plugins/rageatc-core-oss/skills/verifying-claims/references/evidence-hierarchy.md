# Evidence Hierarchy and Weighting

This file provides detailed guidance on applying the primary/secondary/tertiary evidence framework with context-dependent adjustments.

## Overview

Not all sources carry equal weight. The evidence hierarchy provides systematic weighting based on source proximity to original events, research, or data.

**Core principle:** "Always prefer primary sources over secondary sources over tertiary sources."

---

## Tier 1: Primary Sources (Highest Weight)

### Definition

Original documents of events, discoveries, or research. First-hand accounts or original data collection.

### Examples by Domain

**Academic Research:**
- Original peer-reviewed research papers reporting new experiments
- Dissertations and theses (original research)
- Original datasets and raw data
- Laboratory notebooks and research logs
- Clinical trial registries and results

**Government and Statistics:**
- Official statistics from government agencies (ONS, census, Eurostat)
- Primary legislation and regulations
- Court rulings and legal decisions
- Parliamentary records and debates
- Government agency reports (original data collection)

**Historical:**
- Original historical documents and archives
- Letters, diaries, and memoirs (first-hand accounts)
- Photographs and artefacts from the period
- Original recordings or transcripts
- Archaeological evidence

**Corporate and Technical:**
- Patents and technical specifications
- Official company financial statements
- Product documentation from manufacturer
- Original press releases and official statements
- White papers reporting original research or data

**Media:**
- Original investigative reporting with first-hand sources
- Interviews conducted by journalist (original)
- Eyewitness accounts and direct observation
- Original photographs and video

### Weight Assignment

**Multiple primary sources agree:**
- Confidence: 90-100% (Confirmed)
- Condition: Sources are independent and methodologically sound

**Single primary source:**
- Confidence: 70-85% (Likely)
- Condition: Source is high quality but lacks corroboration

**Primary sources disagree:**
- Confidence: Disputed
- Action: Investigate methodological differences, seek additional sources

### Using Primary Sources

**Best practices:**
1. **Always cite primary sources directly** when available
2. **Trace secondary sources back** to primary sources they cite
3. **Verify context** - read full primary source, not just excerpts
4. **Check methodology** - even primary sources can be flawed
5. **Note limitations** - acknowledge what primary source doesn't cover

**Common mistake:** Using newspaper article about ONS statistics instead of citing ONS directly.

**Correct approach:** Locate ONS statistical bulletin and cite that.

## Tier 2: Secondary Sources (High Weight)

### Definition

Analysis, interpretation, or synthesis of primary sources. Provides context, expert interpretation, or synthesis across multiple primary sources.

### Examples by Domain

**Academic:**
- Literature reviews and systematic reviews
- Meta-analyses synthesising multiple studies
- Academic textbooks in subject area
- Review articles in peer-reviewed journals
- Expert commentary on research

**Journalism:**
- Reputable news reporting of facts (Reuters, AP, BBC, The Guardian, Financial Times)
- Investigative journalism citing primary sources
- Fact-checked news analysis
- Documentary journalism with source attribution

**Expert Analysis:**
- Think tank reports citing primary research
- Professional organisation position papers
- Expert testimony based on research
- Policy analysis citing primary data
- Technical analysis by qualified experts

**Reference Works (High-quality):**
- Subject-specific reference books
- Professional handbooks
- Industry reports with original analysis

### Weight Assignment

**Multiple quality secondary sources agree:**
- Confidence: 70-89% (Likely)
- Condition: Sources cite primary evidence and are independent

**Single secondary source:**
- Confidence: 50-69% (Possible)
- Condition: Source is reputable but lacks corroboration

**Secondary sources disagree:**
- Confidence: Disputed or Possible
- Action: Trace back to primary sources to resolve

### Using Secondary Sources

**Appropriate use cases:**
1. **Primary sources inaccessible** - Behind paywalls, in foreign language, or requiring specialised access
2. **Expert interpretation needed** - Technical primary sources require domain expertise to interpret
3. **Synthesis across sources** - Secondary sources provide overview of multiple primary sources
4. **Context and background** - Secondary sources provide historical or theoretical context
5. **Initial orientation** - Use to identify relevant primary sources

**Quality markers for secondary sources:**
- Citations to primary sources provided
- Methodology transparent
- Author expertise in relevant field
- Editorial review or peer review
- Corrections policy exists and is followed

**Red flags:**
- No citations to primary sources
- Anonymous or uncredentialed author
- Contradicts primary sources
- Cherry-picks evidence
- Ignores contradictory research

## Tier 3: Tertiary Sources (Low Weight)

### Definition

Indexes, compilations, or summaries of primary and secondary sources without new analysis or original research.

### Examples

**Reference Works:**
- Encyclopaedias (including Wikipedia)
- Dictionaries (general and subject-specific)
- Almanacs and fact books
- Bibliographies and indexes
- Database aggregators

**Popular Sources:**
- General interest articles
- Listicles and compilations
- Social media posts summarising research
- Infographics (without original data)

### Weight Assignment

**Tertiary sources alone:**
- Confidence: Insufficient for verification
- Standard: "Not acceptable as cited sources in research"

### Appropriate Use

**Acceptable uses:**
1. **Initial orientation** - Get overview of topic
2. **Finding primary/secondary sources** - Follow citations backwards
3. **Quick fact-checking** - Preliminary check before verifying with higher-tier sources
4. **Definitions and terminology** - Standard meanings of terms
5. **Identifying experts and organisations** - Find who are authorities in field

**Unacceptable uses:**
- Final verification of significant claims
- Citation in formal verification report
- Sole source for factual claims

**Wikipedia example:**
- **Don't:** "According to Wikipedia, X is true" (insufficient)
- **Do:** Use Wikipedia to find primary sources, then cite those
- **Do:** "According to [primary source], X is true. See also Wikipedia entry for accessible summary."

## Context-Dependent Adjustments

### When to Increase Source Weight

**Multiple independent sources agree (triangulation):**
- Effect: +10-20 percentage points confidence
- Reason: Consensus across independent sources is strong evidence
- Condition: Sources must be genuinely independent

**Source has strong track record:**
- Effect: +5-10 percentage points
- Indicators: Clean fact-check record, history of corrections when wrong, cited by other experts
- Example: Reuters, AP, ONS have established credibility

**Recency for time-sensitive topics:**
- Effect: +5-10 percentage points for recent sources
- Applies: Fast-moving fields (technology, current events, pandemic science)
- Example: 2025 AI research preferred over 2020 for current capabilities

**Methodology transparent and rigorous:**
- Effect: +5-10 percentage points
- Indicators: Pre-registered studies, open data, reproducible methods, peer-reviewed
- Example: RCTs with pre-registration stronger than observational studies

**Conflicts of interest disclosed:**
- Effect: +5 percentage points
- Reason: Transparency increases trust
- Example: Research noting industry funding and independence provisions

**Within domain of expertise:**
- Effect: +10 percentage points
- Condition: Author/organisation has relevant credentials and experience
- Example: Climate scientist on climate change stronger than general scientist

### When to Decrease Source Weight

**History of errors or corrections:**
- Effect: -10-20 percentage points
- Indicators: Retracted papers, frequent corrections, failed fact-checks
- Action: Verify claims independently even if source seems authoritative

**Undisclosed conflicts of interest:**
- Effect: -15-25 percentage points
- Red flags: Hidden funding, financial interests, personal relationships
- Action: Seek independent sources

**Methodology unclear or flawed:**
- Effect: -20-30 percentage points
- Indicators: No methodology section, non-reproducible, obvious errors
- Action: Do not rely on this source

**Single source without corroboration:**
- Effect: -15-20 percentage points
- Risk: Single-source claims may be errors, outliers, or fabrications
- Action: Seek corroboration before accepting

**Information outdated for domain:**
- Effect: Varies by field (-10 to -50 percentage points)
- Fast-moving fields: Outdated is unreliable
- Historical topics: Older sources may be acceptable
- Action: Check for newer research or data

**Source outside area of expertise:**
- Effect: -20-30 percentage points
- Example: Computer scientist commenting on medieval history
- Action: Find sources within relevant domain

**Poor RADAR scores:**
- High bias: -15-25 percentage points
- Low accuracy track record: -20-30 percentage points
- Not relevant to claim: Exclude entirely
- Anonymous/no credentials: -25-40 percentage points

## Special Cases

### Pre-prints and Non-Peer-Reviewed Research

**Status:** Between Tier 1 and Tier 2

**Considerations:**
- Contains original research (Tier 1 characteristic)
- Lacks peer review (Tier 2 limitation)

**Weight assignment:**
- Confidence: 60-75% (Possible to Likely)
- Increase if: Authors are established experts, methodology is sound, other experts cite it
- Decrease if: Novel claims without corroboration, methodology questionable

**Best practice:**
- Use cautiously
- Note pre-print status explicitly
- Check if peer-reviewed version subsequently published
- Seek corroboration from peer-reviewed sources

### Conflicting Tier Levels

**Scenario:** Tier 1 source disagrees with multiple Tier 2 sources

**Resolution protocol:**
1. Verify all sources are addressing same question (definitional check)
2. Check if Tier 1 source is outdated (temporal check)
3. Examine Tier 2 sources - do they cite different Tier 1 sources?
4. Look for methodological differences
5. Generally prefer Tier 1 if high quality and current
6. Surface disagreement transparently

**Scenario:** Multiple Tier 2 sources agree, contradicting Tier 1 source

**Resolution protocol:**
1. Check if Tier 2 sources are independent (or all citing same flawed Tier 1)
2. Verify Tier 1 source is authoritative and current
3. Look for additional Tier 1 sources
4. If consensus among Tier 2 with multiple Tier 1 sources → weight consensus
5. If Tier 2 all cite single Tier 1 → investigate that Tier 1 source

### Grey Literature

**Definition:** Research and reports outside traditional peer-reviewed channels (government reports, think tank publications, industry white papers, NGO research)

**Tiering:**
- Original data collection by reputable organisation → Tier 1
- Analysis of existing data → Tier 2
- Compilation without analysis → Tier 3

**Weight adjustments:**
- Government agencies with transparent methodology → High weight
- Think tanks with disclosed funding → Moderate weight, check bias
- Industry white papers → Lower weight, check for conflicts of interest
- NGO reports → Moderate weight, assess methodology and bias

## Practical Application

### Verification Workflow Integration

**Phase 2 (Source Discovery):**
- Prioritise finding Tier 1 sources
- Use Tier 3 (Wikipedia) for orientation, then trace to Tier 1/2
- Note tier level for each source discovered

**Phase 3 (Source Quality Assessment):**
- Apply RADAR criteria
- Assign tier level
- Determine context-dependent adjustments

**Phase 4 (Evidence Weighting):**
- Weight evidence by tier + RADAR + context adjustments
- Document weighting rationale

**Phase 5 (Triangulation):**
- Require 3+ sources, preferably Tier 1 or high-quality Tier 2
- Mixed tiers acceptable if Tier 1 sources agree

**Phase 7 (Confidence Calibration):**
- Tier 1 consensus → Confirmed (90-100%)
- Tier 2 consensus → Likely (70-89%)
- Mixed tiers with agreement → Likely (70-85%)
- Tier 3 only → Unverified (insufficient evidence)

### Example Application

**Claim:** "UK unemployment was 4.2% in November 2025"

**Source discovery:**
1. Wikipedia UK Economy page (Tier 3) - mentions unemployment, cites ONS
2. BBC News article (Tier 2) - reports ONS statistics
3. ONS Labour Market Overview November 2025 (Tier 1) - original data

**Weighting:**
- Tier 1 (ONS): 95% base weight (official statistics, primary source)
- RADAR adjustments: +5% (high authority, current, accurate, neutral, relevant)
- Final weight: Very high
- Tier 2 (BBC): 75% base weight (reputable secondary source)
- Check: Does BBC cite ONS correctly? Yes
- Final weight: High (corroboration)
- Tier 3 (Wikipedia): Not weighted for verification (orientation only)

**Triangulation:**
- Primary source (Tier 1) + corroborating secondary (Tier 2) = Sufficient
- Confidence: Confirmed (90-100%)

**Verification output:**
"Confirmed: UK unemployment rate was 4.2% in November 2025 according to ONS Labour Market Overview (primary source). Corroborated by BBC News reporting ONS data."

## Quick Reference

### Source Tiering Decision Tree

1. **Is this original research/data?** → Yes: Tier 1 | No: Continue
2. **Does this analyse/synthesise primary sources?** → Yes: Tier 2 | No: Continue
3. **Is this a compilation/index?** → Yes: Tier 3

### Weight Assignment Quick Guide

**Tier 1 + High RADAR + Current + Corroborated:** 90-100% (Confirmed)
**Tier 1 + Good RADAR + No corroboration:** 70-85% (Likely)
**Tier 2 + High RADAR + Multiple sources agree:** 70-89% (Likely)
**Tier 2 + Good RADAR + Limited corroboration:** 50-69% (Possible)
**Tier 3 only:** Insufficient (Unverified)

### Common Mistakes

- Using Wikipedia as final source (use to find Tier 1/2 instead)
- Citing news article instead of original research it discusses
- Treating all "official" sources as equal (government PR ≠ government statistics)
- Ignoring outdated Tier 1 sources when recent Tier 2 consensus exists
- Accepting single source without checking for corroboration
