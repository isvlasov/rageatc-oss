# Edge Cases and Common Pitfalls

Supplements the edge cases in SKILL.md with harder scenarios and a sweep of common fact-checking mistakes.

## Time-constrained verification

Triage when thorough verification is not possible:

- **Always verify:** high-significance claims affecting key conclusions; high-risk claims where error causes harm; claims contradicting established knowledge; statistics central to the argument.
- **If time permits:** supporting details and low-stakes background claims.
- **Document as unverified:** tangential and low-risk claims; content already marked speculative.

Adjustments: use tertiary sources for orientation only and go straight to primaries for priority claims; accept 2 triangulation sources instead of 3 if both are high-quality Tier 1; lower confidence ratings accordingly (Possible instead of Likely when triangulation is incomplete); and state the constraint in limitations — "Verification conducted under time constraints; lower-priority claims verified with 2 sources instead of the standard 3."

## Paywalled or inaccessible primary sources

In order of preference: institutional or library access and open-access repositories; high-quality secondary sources that read the primary — verify several agree on what it says, and note "primary source inaccessible; verified via [secondaries]"; abstracts, executive summaries, or press releases from the original source (not third-party interpretations); contacting the author (papers are often freely shared) or finding a pre-print.

Never cite a source you have not read or verified through reliable intermediaries, and never keep full confidence when relying on secondaries for an inaccessible primary.

## Ongoing events and predictions

**Ongoing events:** date the verification explicitly — "As of [date/time], [source] reports X"; flag that the situation may change; note the most recent authoritative update; lower confidence for fast-moving situations.

**Predictions:** categorise as Uncheckable — never Confirmed. What can be verified: the predictor's track record and the factual basis underlying the prediction. Maximum confidence Possible or Likely, based on the quality of reasoning and evidence.

## Classified or confidential information

- Sources exist but are classified → Unverified (not Disputed — merely unverifiable); document why verification is impossible; note partial corroboration from declassified or leaked material.
- Whistleblower or leak → assess the source's track record and corroboration; look for indirect verification (public consequences of the claimed secret actions); use cautious confidence language.
- Speculation about classified matters → Uncheckable; do not attempt verification.

## Mutually exclusive claims presented as compatible

"Crime is rising rapidly, and we've never been safer." Identify the specific incompatibility; check whether different metrics (reported crime vs victimisation surveys) or timeframes resolve it; verify each claim separately — both, one, or neither may hold; state the contradiction explicitly, and the resolution if one exists ("resolves if X refers to [context A] and Y to [context B]").

## Statistics without error margins

- Survey data: check or approximate the margin of error from sample size; note its absence in limitations; reduce confidence if the claimed precision exceeds what the method justifies.
- Small differences: flag when within the likely margin — "52% vs 48%" may not be a meaningful difference.
- Comparisons: without significance testing, say "appears to differ", not "differs significantly".

## Common pitfalls

1. **Tertiary-source reliance** — Wikipedia as final verification. Trace to the primary sources it cites and verify there.
2. **Vertical reading** — judging a source by its own About page. Read laterally: search "[source] bias", "[source] reliability", fact-checker assessments.
3. **False balance** — equal space for fringe and consensus positions. Weight by evidence strength and state consensus explicitly ("97%+ of climate scientists…"), rather than presenting outliers as equally credible.
4. **Confidence inflation** — "confirmed" language for Likely-strength evidence. Calibrate language to evidence and preserve uncertainty where it exists.
5. **Hidden disagreements** — quietly picking one of two conflicting authorities. Surface both with attribution and, where determinable, why they differ ("4.2% ILO measure or 3.8% claimant count, depending on definition").
6. **Opinion fact-checking** — attempting to verify "modern art is terrible". Categorise opinions as opinions; verify only their factual premises.
7. **Single-source acceptance** — significant claims need 3 independent sources; a lone source caps confidence at Likely/Possible with the limitation noted.
8. **Context-free statistics** — "unemployment is 4.2%" without measure, population, or date. Verify definitions, methodology, and timeframe; compare like with like; watch for changed measurement standards over time.
9. **Hallucination blindness** — fluent AI output taken as accurate. Verify source grounding systematically (see `hallucination-detection.md`).
10. **Expertise overreach** — definitively judging specialist claims outside your domain. Verify process and provenance (peer-reviewed? credible venue?) and flag the substance for expert review.
11. **Recency bias** — rejecting seminal older work because of its date. Assess currency by field; foundational sources stay relevant; check for supersession rather than assuming it.
12. **Circular citation chains** — three sources citing one original is one source, not triangulation. Verify independence: different organisations, methodologies, and data.

## Escalate immediately

High-stakes specialist medical or legal claims; claims resting on classified information; unresolvable conflict between equally authoritative sources; extraordinary novel research with major implications; methodological disputes beyond general fact-checking; ongoing crises requiring real-time accuracy.
